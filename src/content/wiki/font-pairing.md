Font pairing is the practice of selecting two or more typefaces that complement each other within a composition. The most common frameworks organize pairings by contrast: serif with sans-serif, display with body, or editorial with utilitarian. [50 Best Font Combinations for Graphic Design](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231931-50-best-font-combinations-for-graphic-design) catalogs 50 tested Google Fonts combinations across these categories, offering usage recommendations tied to specific design contexts like editorial layouts, branding, and digital products.

Pairing decisions don't exist in isolation from layout. The Figma micrographics library [Micrographics Templates](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t145719-micrographics-templates-design-layouts) demonstrates that typeface choices interact with surrounding visual density; technical, data-heavy compositions often call for restrained, legible type that doesn't compete with symbolic elements.

Scale is a related variable. [Type Scale Graphs](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-05t183935-type-scale-graphs) from Utopia shows how fluid modular scales affect the size relationships between typographic roles across viewport widths, which in turn shapes how well a pairing holds up at different sizes. A combination that reads clearly at large display sizes may compress poorly on small viewports if the scale ratios aren't considered alongside the typeface choices themselves.
