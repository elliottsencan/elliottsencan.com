Two distinct but reinforcing arguments frame the current debate. [The AI Layoff Trap](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) makes an economics case: when firms shed workers to capture AI cost savings, they also shed the human capital required to supervise and correct those systems, creating a structural trap where short-run savings compound into long-run quality and productivity risks. [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) arrives from a different angle, arguing that Moore's Law trajectories point toward human-level AI around 2040, and that the displacement coming is categorically different from prior automation: previous waves eliminated tasks while creating new job categories; this wave may eliminate entire occupational classes with no equivalent replacement on the near-term horizon. Together the sources suggest the stakes are not just macroeconomic but organizational. Firms must weigh whether layoffs are cost savings or capability destruction.
