Two recurring concerns frame the AI-and-work debate: what happens to displaced workers, and what happens to the firms doing the displacing.

Kevin Drum's [2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argued that Moore's Law puts human-level AI on a plausible trajectory by around 2040, and that this wave differs from prior automation because it threatens cognitive work across the income spectrum, not just routine physical labor. Previous disruptions created new job categories; this one may not, at least not at the same pace or scale.

The firm-level picture is more complicated. Falk and Tsoukalas [argue](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) that companies face a structural trap: cutting headcount to capture AI cost savings also cuts the human capital required to supervise, correct, and improve AI outputs. The short-run savings come at the cost of long-run quality and resilience. Oversight capacity, once shed, is hard to rebuild.
