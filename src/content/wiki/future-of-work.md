Two overlapping concerns define current thinking about AI and employment. The first is structural displacement. [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argues that Moore's Law trajectories point toward human-level AI around 2040, and that unlike earlier automation waves, this one threatens to eliminate entire job classes rather than just shift workers into new roles. The long run may be fine; the transition period is the problem.

The second concern operates at the firm level. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) show that companies face a structural trap: cutting human workers to capture AI cost savings also cuts the oversight capacity needed to catch AI errors. The human capital lost to layoffs is precisely what keeps AI outputs reliable. Short-run savings, long-run fragility.

Together the sources suggest the same dynamic at two scales: automation pressure pushes toward reducing human involvement, but humans remain load-bearing in ways that are easy to undercount until something breaks.
