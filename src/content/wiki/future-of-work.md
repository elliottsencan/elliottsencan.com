Two concerns sit at the center of current thinking about AI and employment. The first is scale and speed. [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argued that Moore's Law trajectories would bring human-level AI around 2040, producing job displacement qualitatively different from earlier automation waves. Previous technological shifts eliminated specific tasks but created new categories of work; this wave, Drum warned, may eliminate entire occupational classes with no replacement in sight before the disruption fully lands.

The second concern is structural, not just transitional. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) argue that firms rushing to cut labor costs by replacing workers with AI are setting a trap for themselves. Human oversight and error-correction capacity erodes when the workforce shrinks, and AI systems depend on that capacity to stay reliable. The short-run cost savings come at the price of long-run quality and productivity. Taken together, these accounts suggest the future-of-work problem is not only about workers displaced by machines but also about whether organizations can maintain the competence to govern the machines they adopt.
