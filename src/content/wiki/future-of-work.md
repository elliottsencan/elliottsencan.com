The future of work sits at the intersection of several forces that rarely get analyzed together: macroeconomic displacement pressure, day-to-day organizational dysfunction, and the structural limits of what machines can actually learn.

On the macroeconomic side, [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) warned that intelligent machines differ from past automation waves because they displace entire categories of labor rather than shifting workers into new sectors. That argument looks more urgent now. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) model a specific mechanism: competitive pressure forces firms to lay off workers before automation's productivity gains are confirmed, producing collectively bad outcomes even when no individual firm acts irrationally. The trap is structural, not a matter of bad intentions.

At the organizational level, the dysfunction runs parallel. [DHg's piece on onboarding](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-08t112608-your-onboarding-is-a-hazing-ritual-and-you-call-it-agile) documents how new hires are set up to fail through meeting-heavy, sink-or-swim practices that masquerade as agile process, while [Abby Malson](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t134831-finite-attention-why-burnout-isnt-your-fault-and-how) argues that on-call systems are designed around data throughput rather than human attention limits, producing burnout that is systemic rather than personal.

What makes these organizational failures consequential for the future of work is what they destroy: tacit knowledge. [Cekrem, drawing on Polanyi](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t110710-the-tacit-dimension-why-your-best-engineers-cant-tell-you), argues that the most valuable engineering expertise, pattern recognition, design intuition, unwritten conventions, can only be transmitted through apprenticeship and is structurally inaccessible to AI. [Abednego Gomes](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-14t223612-the-perils-of-ai-to-the-software-engineering-profession) extends this: shipping AI-generated code without review accelerates skill atrophy and becomes actively dangerous in safety-critical systems. [Tuhin Nair](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-13t060018-why-senior-developers-fail-to-communicate-their-expertise) adds that senior engineers already struggle to make their expertise legible to the business, and that gap, not AI capability, is the real bottleneck.

Ethan Mollick's hands-on report with a frontier agentic system [finds the human role shifting from doing to commissioning](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-09t190614-what-it-feels-like-to-work-with-mythos), which sounds manageable until you ask what commissioning well requires: exactly the tacit judgment that bad onboarding and skill atrophy erode. [Ghost in the Data](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-17t124905-the-competitive-moat-that-ai-cant-replicate) closes the loop from a customer side: organizations that automate away human connection destroy trust that no personalization engine can rebuild. The future of work, across all these sources, is less about what AI can do than about what institutions choose to preserve.
