The central tension in contemporary debates about work and automation is timing. [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argues that Moore's Law trajectories point toward human-level AI around 2040, and that unlike earlier waves of mechanization, this one will permanently eliminate entire job classes rather than just displacing workers into new roles. The near-term disruption, on that account, is not a transition period but a structural shift.

Where Drum focuses on labor markets broadly, [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) identify a more specific trap at the firm level. Companies that lay off workers to capture AI cost savings simultaneously destroy the human capital required to catch AI errors and maintain quality. The oversight capacity that gets cut is exactly what makes AI deployment safe and productive over time. Cost savings in the short run can thus compound into reliability failures in the long run.
