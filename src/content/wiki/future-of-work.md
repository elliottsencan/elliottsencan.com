Kevin Drum's 2013 forecast [in Mother Jones](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argued that Moore's Law puts human-level AI on track for around 2040, and that unlike previous automation waves, this one will permanently eliminate whole categories of work rather than shifting workers into adjacent roles. The near-term disruption is the problem; the long-run robotic abundance, if it arrives, helps no one who loses their livelihood in the transition.

A more recent economics paper [by Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) adds a structural wrinkle. Firms that lay off workers to capture AI cost savings simultaneously destroy the human capital required to oversee and correct those AI systems. The short-run gains come at the cost of long-run quality and reliability, a trap that tightens the more aggressively a firm cuts. Together, these sources frame the future-of-work problem as two-sided: displacement is real and near, but the firms doing the displacing may also be undermining their own productive capacity.
