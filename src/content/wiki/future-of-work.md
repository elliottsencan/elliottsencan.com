Two threads dominate serious thinking about AI and employment. The first is speed and scale: [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argued that Moore's Law trajectories point toward human-level AI around 2040, and that unlike earlier automation waves, this one will eliminate whole occupational categories rather than just reshaping them. Previous disruptions left new job classes in their wake; this one may not.

The second thread is the trap firms walk into when they act on that premise too quickly. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) model a structural problem: cutting workers to capture AI cost savings also destroys the human capital required to catch and correct AI errors. The short-run gain in headcount costs produces long-run fragility in quality and oversight. The workforce AI needs to function well is exactly the one firms are incentivized to eliminate.
