LLM engineering covers the full stack from model weights to production behavior: how models are trained and adapted, how inference is made fast enough and cheap enough to run at scale, and how the harnesses and workflows built around models hold up under real conditions.

On the training and adaptation side, tools like [Unsloth](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-24t093356-unsloth) push fine-tuning into local reach — up to 30x faster training and 90% less memory than FlashAttention 2, with LoRA and FP8 support for over 500 models. The [how-to-train-your-gpt](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-06t173338-raiyanyahyahow-to-train-your-gpt) textbook goes further back, walking through every component of a decoder-only transformer from tokenizer to inference engine. For teams who want domain-specific behavior without full pretraining, [Vibe Training](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-28t140203-vibe-training-auto-train-a-small-language-model-for-your) demonstrates that synthetic data generated by multi-agent debate can produce small classifiers that outperform GPT-4.1 on policy enforcement at a fraction of the cost — a pattern [Plurai](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-04t235011-plurai) productizes with automated data generation and sub-100ms guardrail deployment.

Inference costs and latency are where engineering decisions compound. KV caching treated as a shared data asset — injected via RDMA rather than recomputed — can cut prefill costs by up to 20x according to [Everpure](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-20t073125-how-to-cut-llm-inference-costs-with-kv-caching), with granular-prompt caching extending this further by segmenting prompts into reusable chunks so only changed tokens are processed ([Pure KVA](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-20t073144-maximizing-llm-efficiency-granular-prompt-caching-with-pure)). The [Pragmatic Engineer on inference engineering](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t130559-what-is-inference-engineering) catalogs the broader toolkit: quantization, speculative decoding, parallelism, and disaggregation. At the routing layer, [DigitalOcean's Inference Router](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t192306-how-we-built-digitalocean-inference-router) uses a 30B MoE model to match requests to models by cost, latency, or quality, while [Arch-Router](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t192506-arch-router-aligning-llm-routing-with-human-preferences) achieves similar routing with a compact 1.5B preference-aligned model. Hardware feasibility is its own question: [CanItRun](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-29t173553-canitrun-can-my-gpu-run-this-llm) lets engineers check VRAM compatibility against quantization levels and estimate tokens-per-second before committing.

Reasonableness of model behavior under different effort and budget settings matters as much as raw capability. A benchmark of Claude Opus 4.7 across five reasoning-effort levels found a non-monotonic curve — medium effort won on pass rate and cost-efficiency while higher settings spent more without improving quality ([stet.sh](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-14t190300-opus-47-low-vs-medium-vs-high-vs-xhigh-vs-max-the-reasoning)). On formal specification tasks, frontier models score near-perfect syntax but only around 46% conformance, reciting textbook protocols rather than modeling actual implementations ([SysMoBench](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-08t175639-can-llms-model-real-world-systems-in-tla)).

Agent harness design is now a discipline in its own right. [Harness Design for Long-Running Applications](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-01t104137-harness-design-for-long-running-application-development) describes a GAN-inspired planner-generator-evaluator architecture for multi-hour autonomous coding sessions. The [12-factor-agents](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t174452-humanlayer12-factor-agents) pattern recommends unifying execution and business state into a single context-window-derived thread for simpler serialization, debugging, and recovery. [walkinglabs/learn-harness-engineering](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-18t221205-walkinglabslearn-harness-engineering) structures this into five subsystems: instructions, state, verification, scope, and session lifecycle. Observability without feedback is insufficient; [LangChain's Harrison Chase](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-10t140531-agent-observability-needs-feedback-to-power-learning) argues that attaching feedback signals to traces is what turns monitoring into a learning loop.

Sycophancy is a structural reliability problem, not just a UX quirk. A Bayesian model shows that sycophantic outputs cause delusional belief spiraling even in ideally rational users, and that neither eliminating hallucinations nor warning users fully prevents the effect ([arXiv](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103643-sycophantic-chatbots-cause-delusional-spiraling-even-in)). The analogous problem in knowledge systems: hallucinations baked in at ingest propagate structurally through LLM-compiled wikis, making the lint step non-negotiable ([Building Karpathy's LLM Wiki](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t232201-building-karpathys-llm-wiki-honest-takeaways)).

Cost reduction from AI does not reduce ownership cost. [Yusuf Aytas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-22t091746-when-code-is-cheap-does-quality-still-matter) makes the point that LLMs generate polished technical debt faster than any engineer; taste and judgment remain the scarce resource. [Lars Faye](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-27t145041-agentic-coding-is-a-trap) and The Lobster in the Hot Pot extend this to skills and institutions: dependency on LLMs erodes the engineering knowledge needed to evaluate their output.
