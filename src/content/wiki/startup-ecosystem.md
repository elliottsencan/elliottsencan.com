Failure is structural data. [Startups.RIP](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231537-startupsrip) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies alongside retrospectives and rebuild playbooks, arguing that the ideas outlive the companies that first attempted them. The implication: a graveyard is also a map.

Product philosophy shapes who survives long enough to matter. Paul Buchheit's case in [If your product is Great, it doesn't need to be Good](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t170134-if-your-product-is-great-it-doesnt-need-to-be-good) is that two or three attributes executed exceptionally well beat broad feature coverage every time. Gmail and the iPod are his evidence. Feature completeness is neither necessary nor sufficient.

For AI-native startups, infrastructure costs are the terrain. [The AI Model Pricing War](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-31t072101-the-ai-model-pricing-war-is-here-and-your-margins-depend-on) documents a 75x gap between the cheapest and most expensive frontier models, a collapse in the pricing floor that makes previously unviable business models, freemium, consumer pricing, bulk APIs, now feasible. The prescription is to build provider-agnostic from the start.

But cost compression has a shadow. [The Lobster in the Hot Pot](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103944-the-lobster-in-the-hot-pot) argues that cheap tokens encourage deep workflow dependency, eroding institutional knowledge as they go, and that the NVIDIA-driven investment cycle is inflating a bubble whose pop will arrive as a cost shock. Companies built on current pricing assumptions may find the floor gone.

At the infrastructure layer, [SpaceX and the Sentient Sun](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t231454-spacex-and-the-sentient-sun) frames SpaceX as a case study in stacking compounding advantages, Starlink revenue, reusability cost curves, and Starship's economics, toward an outcome that reshapes what infrastructure even means for the next generation of companies.
