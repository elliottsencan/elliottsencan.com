The health of the startup ecosystem is partly a function of what failures reveal. [Startups.RIP](https://startups.rip/) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies with the argument that ideas outlive the companies that first attempt them; the graveyard is a resource, not just a record. Failed startups expose which problems remain unsolved and which market conditions weren't yet ready.

Cost structure is another determinant. The 75x pricing spread between cheap models like Grok and premium ones like Claude Opus has, according to [Ayush Chaturvedi](https://superframeworks.com/articles/ai-model-pricing-war-indie-hackers), opened business models that were unprofitable at 2025 rates. Freemium and consumer-facing AI products now have room to exist. The flip side, raised by [Christoph Spörk](https://opententacle.com/article/2026-04-05/), is that current token prices may not reflect real infrastructure costs, and a correction driven by NVIDIA's investment cycle could cripple companies that built assuming cheap inference would persist.

At the infrastructure layer, [a16z's profile of SpaceX](https://www.a16z.news/p/spacex-and-the-sentient-sun) illustrates a pattern that recurs across ecosystems: a foundational company whose unit economics improve through iteration — Falcon 9 reusability, Starlink revenue, Starship cost reduction — eventually enables entirely new categories of startups built on top of it.

At the product level, [Paul Buchheit's 2010 argument](https://paulbuchheit.blogspot.com/2010/02/if-your-product-is-great-it-doesnt-need.html) that great products need only nail two or three attributes exceptionally well still shapes how early-stage teams prioritize. Feature completeness is not the goal; a few things done uncommonly well tend to matter more than broad adequacy.
