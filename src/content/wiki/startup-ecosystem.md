Failed startups leave behind more than cautionary tales. [Startups.RIP](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231537-startupsrip) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies alongside retrospectives and rebuild playbooks, making the case that ideas outlive the companies that first attempt them. The implication for founders is practical: the graveyard is a research tool, not a memorial.

The infrastructure layer underneath new startups is under its own pressure. [Christoph Spörk](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103944-the-lobster-in-the-hot-pot) argues that deep LLM integration erodes institutional knowledge gradually, and that the NVIDIA-driven investment cycle inflating AI capacity today sets up a cost shock when token prices rise. Startups built on cheap inference may find their unit economics collapse before they reach scale.

The counterargument comes from the pricing side. [Ayush Chaturvedi](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-31t072101-the-ai-model-pricing-war-is-here-and-your-margins-depend-on) documents a 75x spread between the cheapest and most expensive frontier models, a gap that makes business models viable now that were unprofitable at 2025 rates. The practical recommendation is to build provider-agnostic from day one, treating the pricing war as a structural opportunity rather than a temporary discount.
