The graveyard is instructive. [Startups.RIP](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231537-startupsrip) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies with the argument that failed ideas outlive the companies that first tried them. The implication: the ecosystem does not discard problems when a startup dies, it recycles them toward the next attempt.

Capital narratives shape which attempts get funded. A16z's profile of SpaceX [frames the company](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t231454-spacex-and-the-sentient-sun) as infrastructure for a multiplanetary future, with Starlink revenue subsidizing the cost reductions that make Mars colonies conceivable. That framing is characteristic of how large venture firms manufacture conviction around platform bets: the story of civilization-scale impact attracts capital that in turn makes the infrastructure real.

The AI layer introduces a different dynamic. Christoph Spörk [warns](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103944-the-lobster-in-the-hot-pot) that companies building on cheap LLM tokens are accumulating a hidden liability: as NVIDIA-driven investment props up artificially low pricing, the institutional knowledge to work without AI erodes. When token costs surge, companies will lack both the margin and the capability to absorb the shock. Ayush Chaturvedi [sees the same pricing gap differently](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-31t072101-the-ai-model-pricing-war-is-here-and-your-margins-depend-on), arguing that a 75x spread between providers has opened business models that were simply unavailable at 2025 rates. The disagreement is about timing and dependency: one treats cheap tokens as a trap, the other as a window.

At the product level, Paul Buchheit's observation [holds](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t170134-if-your-product-is-great-it-doesnt-need-to-be-good): startups that survive tend to do two or three things exceptionally well and ignore the rest. The ecosystem rewards that kind of selective excellence more reliably than it rewards completeness.
