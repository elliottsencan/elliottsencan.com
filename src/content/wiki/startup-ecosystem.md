Most startup ideas don't die with the companies that first attempted them. [Startups.RIP](https://startups.rip/) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies alongside retrospectives and rebuild playbooks, treating failed startups as proof-of-concept rather than proof-of-impossibility. The implication is that the ecosystem recycles ideas across waves of founders, each inheriting a clearer picture of what the previous attempt got wrong.

The current wave is being reshaped by AI infrastructure economics. A 75x pricing gap between the cheapest and most expensive frontier models has collapsed the cost floor that previously made freemium and consumer-tier AI products unworkable [The AI Model Pricing War](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-31t072101-the-ai-model-pricing-war-is-here-and-your-margins-depend-on). But [Christoph Spörk](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103944-the-lobster-in-the-hot-pot) warns that the same pricing environment is fragile: a circular NVIDIA-driven investment bubble is suppressing token prices artificially, and a cost shock could gut the margins that new business models are being built on. Building provider-agnostic from day one is the hedge.

At the product level, Paul Buchheit's argument from Gmail's early days still holds: a product that does two or three things exceptionally well can ignore everything else and still win [If your product is Great](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t170134-if-your-product-is-great-it-doesnt-need-to-be-good). Feature completeness is not the threshold. That framing aligns with how a16z reads SpaceX: Starlink revenue and Falcon 9 reusability were not adjacent features but the specific bets that funded everything downstream [SpaceX & the Sentient Sun](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-21t231454-spacex-and-the-sentient-sun). Concentrated excellence in a few axes, with deliberate neglect of others, appears across very different kinds of companies.
