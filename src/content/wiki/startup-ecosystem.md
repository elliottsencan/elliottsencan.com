Failed startups leave behind more than cautionary tales. [Startups.RIP](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231537-startupsrip) catalogs over 1,700 dead YC companies alongside retrospectives and rebuild playbooks, making the case that the ideas themselves outlive the companies that first attempted them. Failure, in this framing, is a data point the ecosystem can recycle.

The current AI investment cycle adds structural risk to that picture. [The Lobster in the Hot Pot](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-03t103944-the-lobster-in-the-hot-pot) argues that a circular NVIDIA-driven bubble is inflating AI infrastructure spending while companies quietly shed institutional knowledge by offloading cognitive work to LLMs. If token prices surge once the investment subsidies end, companies that built deep dependencies will face a cost shock with diminished internal capacity to adapt.

That risk has a near-term counterweight. A 75x pricing gap between the cheapest and most expensive frontier models has collapsed the AI cost floor, opening up freemium, consumer, and bulk API business models that were unprofitable at 2025 rates. [Ayush Chaturvedi](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-31t072101-the-ai-model-pricing-war-is-here-and-your-margins-depend-on) argues the defensive move is to build provider-agnostic from day one, so that margin exposure shifts with the market rather than locking into a single vendor's pricing trajectory. The two views sit in tension: low prices today make AI-native startups viable, but the same dependency that makes them possible is what Spörk warns will become a liability when prices revert.
