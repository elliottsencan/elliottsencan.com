Two concerns recur across the sources here: whether automation eliminates jobs in ways past technological shifts did not, and whether firms that automate fastest may be undermining themselves.

[Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argues that AI-driven automation differs from earlier waves because it threatens cognitive and white-collar work, not just physical or routine tasks. Moore's Law projections put human-level AI around 2040, and Drum's concern is the transition period: entire job categories disappear faster than labor markets can absorb displaced workers.

[Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) formalize a related but distinct problem. Firms that lay off workers to capture AI cost savings destroy the human capital required to supervise and correct AI outputs. The short-run efficiency gain inverts into a long-run quality and reliability risk. The trap is structural: once that oversight capacity is gone, rebuilding it is expensive and slow.
