Two distinct anxieties run through recent writing on automation. The first is macroeconomic: unlike earlier waves of mechanization that displaced some jobs while creating others, the coming wave of AI-driven automation may eliminate entire occupational categories with no equivalent replacement. [Kevin Drum's 2013 essay](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) anchors this in Moore's Law, arguing that exponential compute growth puts human-level AI in reach by around 2040 and that the economic disruption in the intervening decades will fall hardest on workers whose jobs can be codified and delegated to software.

The second anxiety is organizational. Even if AI reliably handles routine tasks, firms face a structural trap when they respond to automation by cutting the human workforce that monitors it. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) model this directly: laying off workers to capture short-run cost savings erodes the human capital required to oversee and correct AI outputs, creating long-run quality and productivity risks that offset the apparent gains. The two concerns compound each other; the same cost pressure that drives firms toward aggressive headcount cuts also degrades their ability to catch the failures automation introduces.
