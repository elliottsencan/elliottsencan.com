Two threads run through the current debate on automation. The first is structural displacement. [Kevin Drum's 2013 piece](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-28t074225-welcome-robot-overlords-please-dont-fire-us) argues that unlike earlier waves of mechanization, AI-driven automation follows Moore's Law toward human-level capability by around 2040, eliminating entire job categories rather than just shifting workers into adjacent roles. Previous automation displaced physical labor while expanding demand for cognitive work; this wave targets cognitive work directly, leaving fewer adjacent roles to absorb displaced workers.

The second thread is a governance problem that emerges once automation is already deployed. [Falk and Tsoukalas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t155432-cognitive-offloading-and-ai-how-reliance-on-llms-affects) describe a structural trap: firms cut labor costs by laying off workers, but those workers carried the institutional knowledge needed to supervise and correct AI outputs. The short-run savings come at the cost of long-run quality and reliability, because the human capital required for oversight has been eliminated. The two risks compound each other — displacement reduces the workforce, and reduced oversight degrades the systems that replaced them.
