Software engineering is not a single skill but a cluster of practices held together by a shared concern: shipping systems that work reliably, remain maintainable, and serve people who depend on them. The sources here cover that cluster from multiple angles, and several recurring tensions emerge.

**Design and structure.** Good module design reduces the cognitive surface area that any one contributor needs to hold in mind. [AI Likes Deep Modules](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-04t231343-ai-likes-deep-modules) argues that small interfaces hiding large implementations benefit both human readers and LLMs asked to work with a codebase. The Single Responsibility Principle is frequently cited as a guide to this, but [Single Responsibility, the Distorted Principle](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-04t073318-single-responsibility-the-distorted-principle) argues the principle is widely misread: it does not mean "do only one thing" but rather that a unit should have one accountable reason to change, and over-granularizing classes can make a system harder to understand, not easier.

**Testing and quality.** Tests are only valuable if they survive the churn of real development. [Designing Playwright Tests That Survive UI Refactors](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-05t135218-designing-playwright-tests-that-survive-ui-refactors) argues that suites break not because selectors are wrong but because tests couple to implementation details rather than semantic roles. When AI writes those tests, the problem compounds: [Code Smells when you get AI to write your Frontend Tests](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t185420-code-smells-when-you-get-ai-to-write-your-frontend-tests) documents over twenty recurring patterns including over-mocking and tests written to match a buggy implementation rather than intended behavior. Code quality does not become less important when generation is cheap. [When Code Is Cheap, Does Quality Still Matter?](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-22t091746-when-code-is-cheap-does-quality-still-matter) points out that LLMs can produce polished, well-formatted technical debt faster than any individual engineer, so taste and judgment remain the binding constraint.

**Security.** Supply chain risk is real and not just theoretical. [Supply-chain attack using invisible Unicode code hits GitHub](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231634-supply-chain-attack-using-invisible-code-hits-github-and) describes 151 malicious npm packages encoding payloads in invisible Unicode variation-selector characters, bypassing both code review and static analysis. Even with powerful AI-assisted analysis tools, [Approaching zero bugs?](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t094735-approaching-zero-bugs) finds no measurable signal in curl's data that open-source projects are converging on zero latent vulnerabilities.

**The AI question.** This is where the sources are in most active tension with each other. [Agentic Coding is a Trap](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-27t145041-agentic-coding-is-a-trap) argues that full agentic workflows accelerate skill atrophy, invert developer priorities toward speed over understanding, and create dependency on AI providers. [The Perils of "AI" to the Software Engineering Profession](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-14t223612-the-perils-of-ai-to-the-software-engineering-profession) adds that shipping AI-generated code without review is categorically incompatible with safety-critical systems. Val Town's [Slow Mode](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t193626-slow-mode) proposes a middle path: an agent that keeps the human involved at every step, trading short-term productivity for genuine learning. On the other side, [Formal Methods and the Future of Programming](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-15t021106-formal-methods-and-the-future-of-programming) argues that agentic coding has made formal verification newly cost-effective by lowering the cost of writing proofs, suggesting the relationship between AI and engineering rigor is not purely subtractive. [The bottleneck was never the code](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-06t110728-the-bottleneck-was-never-the-code) reframes the whole debate: coding agents make code-writing cheap, but the real bottleneck is organizational — shared context, specification clarity, and management coherence.

**Tacit knowledge and expertise.** What AI cannot easily replicate is the subject of [The Tacit Dimension](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t110710-the-tacit-dimension-why-your-best-engineers-cant-tell-you), which draws on Polanyi to argue that the most valuable engineering expertise — pattern recognition, design intuition, unwritten conventions — is structurally inaccessible to AI tools and can only be transmitted through apprenticeship. [Why Senior Developers Fail to Communicate Their Expertise](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-13t060018-why-senior-developers-fail-to-communicate-their-expertise) identifies a related gap: senior engineers frame their value in terms of complexity management while the rest of the business thinks in terms of uncertainty reduction, and bridging that translation is itself a core engineering skill.

**Hiring and interviews.** The interview processes meant to filter for engineering talent are themselves broken. [Learn Algorithms for Interviews, Forget Them for Work](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t155134-learn-algorithms-for-interviews-forget-them-for-work) argues algorithm tests measure a narrow trainable skill that weakly correlates with production performance. [The systemic decay of tech hiring](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t182141-the-systemic-decay-of-tech-hiring) traces how error asymmetry drives ever-harder tests, diffused interviewer accountability, and Goodhart's Law dynamics that reward overfit candidates.

**Operational practice.** Production experience informs the [Unwritten Laws of Software Engineering](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-10t073045-the-unwritten-laws-of-software-engineering): roll back before debugging, treat every external dependency as a future outage. Understanding distributed systems in production means learning to read traces; How to read distributed traces when you didn't write the code provides the anatomy. CI at scale presents its own challenges: [What CI Actually Looks Like at a 100-Person Team](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t195531-what-ci-actually-looks-like-at-a-100-person-team) describes how 575K weekly jobs and 33M test executions require an AI triage agent just to keep the signal legible.

Across all of this, the discipline rewards people who build mental models, not just people who produce output. That constraint predates AI and does not disappear because generation is cheap.
