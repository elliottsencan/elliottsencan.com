Software engineering resists clean definition, and the sources collected here collectively resist any single thesis about what it is or where it's headed. What they share is a concern with the gap between producing code and building software that actually works, lasts, and can be understood by the next person who touches it.

The most persistent thread is that writing code is not the bottleneck. [The Typical Set](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-06t110728-the-bottleneck-was-never-the-code) argues directly that the real constraints are organizational: shared context, specification clarity, and management coherence. Coding agents may make individual code-writing cheap, but they amplify whatever alignment or misalignment already exists. [Yusuf Aytas](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-22t091746-when-code-is-cheap-does-quality-still-matter) makes the same point from a different angle: AI lowers the cost of producing code but not the cost of owning it, and LLMs can generate polished technical debt faster than any individual engineer ever could. Taste and judgment remain the scarce inputs.

Where sources diverge is on how AI tools interact with engineering skill. Lars Faye [argues](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-27t145041-agentic-coding-is-a-trap) that full agentic workflows accelerate skill atrophy and invert developer priorities toward speed over understanding, recommending LLMs as secondary delegation tools rather than primary authors. Abednego Gomes [frames](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-14t223612-the-perils-of-ai-to-the-software-engineering-profession) "vibe coding" as categorically incompatible with safety-critical systems. Pete Millspaugh [proposes](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t193626-slow-mode) a "Slow Mode" agent that keeps the human involved at every step, trading short-term throughput for genuine ownership. Armin Ronacher [warns](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-23t161552-the-coming-loop) that harness loops orchestrating agents risk producing codebases that require machine participation to maintain. On the other side, Anthropic engineers [describe](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t221035-effective-harnesses-for-long-running-agents) practical multi-context agent harnesses that enable real progress; Yaron Minsky [argues](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-15t021106-formal-methods-and-the-future-of-programming) agentic coding has made formal verification newly cost-effective by lowering the cost of writing proofs while simultaneously creating demand for verification beyond what tests alone provide.

Skill atrophy has a parallel in hiring. Fagner Brack [argues](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t155134-learn-algorithms-for-interviews-forget-them-for-work) algorithm interviews test a narrow, trainable skill that weakly correlates with production performance; real engineering requires reading tradeoffs, shipping incrementally, and handling messy, unbounded real-world inputs. Vladimir Klepov [traces](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t182141-the-systemic-decay-of-tech-hiring) how error asymmetry drives ever-harder interview processes, with candidates overfitting via Goodhart's Law. Both point to institutional structures that optimize for the wrong thing.

Design principles sit beneath all tooling debates. Go Monk [argues](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-04t231343-ai-likes-deep-modules) that deep modules, small interfaces hiding large implementations, reduce complexity and make systems easier for both humans and LLMs to understand. Henrique Teixeira [corrects](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-04t073318-single-responsibility-the-distorted-principle) the common misreading of the Single Responsibility Principle: it means cohesive grouping under a single accountable responsibility, not atomizing classes until they lose cognitive coherence. Anton Zaides [distills](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-10t073045-the-unwritten-laws-of-software-engineering) hard-won production rules, including rolling back before debugging and treating every external dependency as a future outage.

Quality signals and testing appear across multiple sources. Daniel Stenberg [uses](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-02t094735-approaching-zero-bugs) curl's vulnerability data to argue there is no measurable sign yet that AI-assisted static analysis is pushing open-source projects toward zero latent bugs. The Currents team [argues](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-05t135218-designing-playwright-tests-that-survive-ui-refactors) that test suites break during refactors because they couple to implementation details rather than semantic roles. AI-generated tests introduce their own problems: [How To Test Frontend](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-22t185420-code-smells-when-you-get-ai-to-write-your-frontend-tests) documents patterns like over-mocking and testing buggy implementations rather than intended behavior. Imbue's experiment [finds](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-23t212958-how-ai-code-review-can-make-correct-code-worse) that AI reviewer-fixer pipelines can break correct code when the fixer overreaches beyond review scope.

Expertise transmission is its own problem. Tuhin Nair [observes](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-13t060018-why-senior-developers-fail-to-communicate-their-expertise) that senior developers communicate in terms of complexity management while the rest of the business thinks in terms of uncertainty reduction. Cekrem [draws on Polanyi](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-19t110710-the-tacit-dimension-why-your-best-engineers-cant-tell-you) to argue that the most valuable engineering expertise, pattern recognition and design intuition, is structurally inaccessible to AI tools and can only be transmitted through apprenticeship. Poor onboarding [compounds this](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-08t112608-your-onboarding-is-a-hazing-ritual-and-you-call-it-agile): meeting-heavy, probation-enforced onboarding systematically prevents knowledge transfer while making the dysfunction invisible to management.

At the infrastructure layer, the discipline still rewards understanding the underlying system. Ivan Velichko [walks through](/reading/2026-05/2026-05-04t231858-how-container-filesystem-works-building-a-docker-like) assembling a container from scratch using Linux namespaces, mount propagation, and pivot_root. Supply-chain attacks using invisible Unicode characters [demonstrate](/reading/2026-04/2026-04-30t231634-supply-chain-attack-using-invisible-code-hits-github-and) that tooling assumptions can be exploited at layers reviewers never look at. Colin Breck [reminds](/reading/2026-06/2026-06-30t185207-when-impressive-performance-gains-do-not-matter) that even order-of-magnitude improvements fail to change outcomes when attention thresholds, capacity increments, or pipeline backpressure constrain the real bottleneck.

The Anton Zaides framing of ["builders" versus "keepers"](/reading/2026-07/2026-07-07t170607-the-software-engineering-war) captures the current cultural fault line: engineers who prioritize shipping fast with AI versus those who insist on code quality. The sources here suggest both camps are responding to real constraints, and that the discipline's challenge is holding both simultaneously.
